Aggressive Weight Cuts in Main Events.

Main events can last for five long rounds for some fighters. Athletes who cut too much weight often find themselves in tough grappling or wrestling contests and quickly become fatigued.

This could be the game plan of their opponent, who knows that if they drag their opponent into rounds four and five, they can easily take the fight away from them by tiring their bigger opponent out.

Motherhood and Fighting.

Female fighters with children might find it very difficult to balance their personal and professional lives. While their bodies may recover easily, the mental strain can be challenging

Knockout Losses and Weak Chins

Suffering several KO losses can lead to a weak chin. Many fighters develop what is known as a "glass chin" and can only take one or two good shots before crumbling to the canvas. All the damage from their previous bouts adds up over time, and these injuries can affect a fighter's performance. Usually, fighters who have been in some wars (wild fights or fighting recklessly) tend to fall into this category.

Biased Judges

Judges in certain cities, such as Las Vegas, may have biases compared to judges from other countries or commissions. Whenever a fight goes to decision in Vegas, be prepared for a potentially controversial outcome. Two different judges may interpret the same fight in different ways, leading to accusations of robbery. Keep an eye on judges with a history of scoring matchups incorrectly under the current point system, as their names often come up in news articles.

Bad Referees

Organizations typically have their own staple referees, but sometimes a new and inexperienced referee is assigned to a match. Unfortunately, as a punter, you may not know who the referee will be for a specific match. Some referees may make mistakes, such as stopping a fight too early, which is out of your control. Keep an eye out for referees with a history of bad decisions and tread lightly if you know they will be the referee for the match you bet on. Additionally, different commissions have their own sets of referees, so you may see an unfamiliar referee in a different state or country.

Weight Cuts

Bad weight cuts often lead to poor performances on fight night. They can result from illness or bad dieting, and if a fighter misses weight or struggles to make weight in the weeks leading up to a bout, it could potentially put the bout in danger of being called off. Look out for fighters with a history of bad weight cuts, as they tend not to perform as well in their bouts.

Overweight

Some fighters receive hefty penalties for being overweight, which usually results in them losing a portion of their purse. This fact can put extra mental pressure on a fighter and give extra motivation to their opponent going into a fight. Being significantly overweight is seen as unprofessional, and fights may be made into catchweight bouts or canceled altogether.

Bulky fighters

Muscular fighters and their gas tanks (cardio) don't last in later rounds. Big muscles require more blood, which in turn needs more oxygen to carry to different parts of the body. Keep an eye out for these types of fighters. Usually, a grueling weight cut with not enough time to recover leads to a poor performance. These type of fighters tend to perform well for only one round but in later rounds, their cardio lets them down, and fatigue takes over

Injuries

Undisclosed injuries that are only revealed after the fight can significantly affect a fighter's performance. Unfortunately, you won't know what injury a fighter has because it is kept secret. Pay close attention to when fighters undergo surgery and how quickly they return to the octagon. Returning too soon might not be a good thing. Make notes of when a fighter gets injured, write down any updates, and note how long they have been off. Fighting too soon might not be the best idea, especially if the fighter is in the later stages of their career.

Suspension

Suspended fighters and time off can affect their performance in the octagon. Some fighters are just not the same as they used to be after a long layoff. Whether the suspension was drug-related or injury-related, keep an eye on how quickly or slowly the fighter makes a comeback and who they are making a comeback against.

Short Notice

Fighters who take fights on short notice or stand in for injured fighters might have a disadvantage because they don't have a full fight camp to get into optimal shape for the fight. Look closely at the odds for fighters who stand in. Usually, they will be lower-ranked fighters or new fighters to the organization trying to prove themselves.With higher ranked opponents that stand in to fight be extra careful when considering to bet on them.

Young Fighters

Young fighters who stay active and fight regularly without taking too much damage tend to have a longer career. They build experience and often change weight classes. These types of fighters are usually tough matchups for newcomers and often surprise when stepping in as replacement fighters on short notice or in main events.

Turbulent Events

Every now and then, events leading up to a fight cause major changes in how things were planned. Some press conferences get canceled, and some lose their main events due to bad weight cuts. When there is a major reshuffle between fighters and their opponents on a card, be cautious when betting because bookmakers scramble to make accurate lines.

Social Media

Follow fighters on Instagram and see what they post. Here and there, valuable information might be dropped, or they might say something that could sway your bet to the other side. Sometimes their managers drop info that was not suppose to be leaked.

Wrestling

Wrestling is a great skill advantage to have, and usually, with wrestling, comes great cardio. Controlling the fight on the ground and making your opponent tired with relentless pressure breaks a fighter's will. Usually, fighters and fans get frustrated, but winning is winning, no matter what the game plan is. Some of the best champions always have well-rounded wrestling skills in their arsenal.

Submission Artists

A great set of jiu-jitsu skills, just like wrestling, is invaluable to a fighter. Early rounds, where sweat does not play a big factor in getting out of submissions, are advantageous to fighters who don't mind fighting off their backs or going for an early submission. Look out for fighters with a great grappling history and, more importantly, how they match up with opponents.

Cardio

The cornerstone of a great fighter is cardio. The better the fighter's cardio, the longer they last inside the cage. Fighters who have fought championship level with five rounds know that cardio sometimes gets you the win in the later rounds if the other fighter doesn't have the experience of fighting five rounds. Look out for fighters with a strong winning record and who have also fought in rounds three to five.

Switch Stance

Fighters who have the ability to fight in both southpaw and orthodox stances tend to be more unpredictable. Look out for these types of fighters, as they impose a guessing game on their opponents and can catch them with sneaky strikes that add up in the later rounds.

Karate Stance

Karate stance fighters, also known as point strikers, are tricky to fight against. They are very good at keeping their distance and outscoring their opponents. Opponents tend to get frustrated and deviate from their game plan, which can lead to getting caught and ending the fight. However, these fighters can struggle when taken down and fighting from the ground. Additionally, a weak point of this stance is the exposure of the front leg to leg kicks. The more damage the front leg takes from effective front kicks, the less mobility the fighter will have, and switching their stance may not be optimal.

Competition Level

The level of competition inside training camps and gyms is important. Fighters often become a product of their environment. Look at who the fighter is training with and how they fare in the UFC. Some gyms are known for producing absolute monsters, so keep an eye out for fighters who are coming out of very successful gyms.

Analysts, Punters, Gurus, and Experts

Follow other analysts and listen to what they have to say, but ultimately, stick with your own decisions and analysis. Two key lessons to learn from following other punters' advice or information are that overconfident picks cannot always be considered truthful information, and sometimes, when someone's overconfident picks do become true (especially parlays), the punter is mistaken for an expert. Be aware of punters who are affiliated with bookies.

Records

Padded records might make a fighter look great, but it's important to consider the level of competition they faced in previous organizations. If a new prospect is making their debut, check the level of competition they fought previously. Add their 5-10 previous opponents' wins together and do the same with their losses. Divide the wins by the total fights and see how successful all of them were. This is just a general gauge for you to see how strong previous competitors were. Fight records are important, but don't get fooled by them.

Contracts

Fighters under pressure after a certain amount of losses usually get matched up with fighters in a similar situation. You can expect a banger of a fight because there is so much at stake for both fighters

Coaches

The biggest downfall of a fighter can be their coach and what they believe. Stubborn, one-dimensional, or yes-men coaches have detrimental consequences for many fighters' careers before they realize it's too late.

Fight IQ

Fighter IQ is something to take note of when fighters compete. Some have the opportunity or multiple opportunities to end a fight but end up losing. Take note of what commentators are saying. Sometimes it's obvious to see a fighter is making the wrong decisions by not listening to their coach's advice or not changing their game plan.

Hype

Overhyped fighters can cause the betting lines of the favorite not to move much, making it a good time to bet on the favorite. Additionally, popular athletes with large fan bases can contribute to the hype of a fighter, making them seem better than their true skills. This can happen when a new fighter fights only once and immediately gets a title shot.

Inactivity

Extended periods of inactivity can cause fighters to fall behind in the sport's evolution. Many fighters step away for two years or more for various reasons, but the sport continues to progress, and athletes become better and better. When a fighter returns, they may struggle to keep up with the evolving skills of their competitors.

Retirement

Fighters who retire and then decide to return may not be making the best decision. Although some fighters continue training, it's impossible to accurately predict what level they'll be when facing high-level competition again. Age also plays a role, and failing to keep up with the younger generation can be a significant mistake for fighters.

Age

Younger fighters tend to perform better than older fighters if the age gap is 5 years or more between the two athletes. Younger fighters are more hungry; they want to prove a point, climb the ranks, and recover quicker from injuries. They also have more fighting years ahead of them. Older fighters have experience on their side, but if an older fighter misses weight or their weight cut does not go well, or they have been inactive for a long period of time, the odds might favor the younger fighter. Fighter age and record are probably the most important factors to consider when choosing a winner. Fighters between the ages of 25 to early 30s tend to be in their prime.

Experience

A solid winning fight record with an active fighter is a solid foundation to look at. An athlete with at least two fights a year or more is an active fighter. Some fighters learn from past experiences and adapt their fighting styles accordingly or adjust to a different style depending on their opponent. It is essential to know what kind of style a fighter uses to compete.

Fighting Style

Matchmakers are excellent at pairing up fighters with similar styles and records, making it hard for punters to select a winner. Sometimes there are exceptions when wrestlers are matched against strikers. This is an important factor to keep in mind, and stats like take-down defensive and accuracy play a significant role. If fighters like to strike, don't be surprised if one comes up with a more wrestling plan during a fight. Take-downs can easily win a round for the more active fighter during a fight. Grapplers tend not to engage in striking contests but rather grind out a win through wrestling and take-downs.

How fights end

When looking at prop bets, understanding how fighters win fights can be helpful. By analyzing past events and how fighters have finished fights, you can gain an indication of how a fight might end. Typically, fighters will utilize their strongest skill to finish the fight. For example, heavyweight fighters often rely on striking to secure a quick knockout.

Press conferences and weigh-ins:

If possible, watch press conferences and weigh-ins and read the comment section. By doing so, you might pick up on something that others have noticed. Reading comments on these events can also provide an indication of the direction in which the public's money is flowing before a fight.

Check bookmaker's odds well in advance:

Always take note of the money lines on certain matchups and how they are moving. Understanding how odds work and who sets the lines is also important. If you have an idea, be quick to place a wager well in advance.

Big favorites:

Betting on heavy favorites is not always the best idea as they don't provide much value, and upsets are always possible. It might not even be worth including them in a parlay, especially in the main and co-main events where huge upsets tend to happen.

Optimal lines:

Look for lines that are +100 or better, particularly when champions are the underdog or when excellent fighters are on the wrong side of the line, as this provides a lot of value. This means that you need to be well aware of who is fighting whom when fights are announced.

Over-under in main events:

In big occasions with 5 rounds per fight, better fighters more often than not go the distance.

Women's fights:

Predicting women's fights can be challenging due to their unpredictable performance and fighting styles. Conduct thorough research on past fights and opponents.

Strikes absorbed per minute

A fighter with poor defense or who fights recklessly might have a high stat of strikes absorbed per minute. Taking too much damage in a fight can sway the decision to the other fighter, even if the fighter had more control time in the fight. Look out for fighters with high numbers in this category as it usually means their defense is not strong enough, and against a seasoned striker in later rounds, they fail to persist.

Popularity and priorities

The more popular a fighter becomes, the busier their life becomes. Monetary offers may cross their career paths, and the financial benefits of these deals are often too good to pass up. This causes fighters to lose focus and hunger for fighting, leading to difficulties with up-and-coming fighters who want to take their spot or title. These fighters find it challenging to balance everything and have a hard time maintaining their position in the fighting world."

Cancelled Fights

If a fight is cancelled for any reason and you have placed a bet on it, don't feel obligated to place another bet on a different fight. Instead, it's better to keep your money and bet on another day.

Protected Fighters

You can often identify a "protected fighter" when an organization views them as a cash cow and gives them "easy" fights to pad their record. Typically, over-hyped fighters fall into this category as well, but they will eventually meet their match when they least expect it. Keep track of each of their fights, where they fight, and you will notice a weak point somewhere. Social media posts by fans can also reveal information about these types of fighters.

Mis-matched Bouts

Be cautious when betting on the odds for these types of fights. Usually, there is a significant age difference or experience deficit between fighters, but the odds do not reflect this. It may be tempting to place a bet, but bookies make these bets appear attractive.

Events in Cities with High Elevation

Pay attention to when athletes travel to these cities for their bouts. Some athletes arrive a month early to acclimate to higher elevation areas. Higher elevations place extra strain on an individual's cardio. The more muscular a fighter is and the higher the elevation, the more difficult it is for that athlete to push themselves, particularly in later rounds. Look out for fighters who travel on short notice and did a full camp at much lower elevations. They could potentially be at a disadvantage.

Your Notes

Take notes during events and organize them. Many announcers drop useful information, and interesting stats are shown on screen. Write them down next to a fighter's name. Also, jot down your own observations, such as if a fighter gasses easily, is a slow starter, lowers their hands too often, is a weak grappler, a sloppy striker, or is stressed.
